Roles and Responsibilities

In the Scam Investigation Department, we operate with four distinct roles. Each position carries specific responsibilities and plays a vital part in fulfilling our mission: identifying scammers, reducing scam incidents, and maintaining the integrity of the RoDevs community.
Trial Scam Investigator (TSI)
The role of a TSI is to learn the department inside and out. This is a training position where members begin developing their investigative skills, gaining hands on experience, and learning things that will prepare them for promotion to full Scam Investigator. Asking questions, listening to seniors, and actively participating in training are essential at this stage.
Scam Investigator (SI)
SI‚Äôs are the primary case handlers of the department. Their responsibilities include investigating scam cases, reviewing ban appeals, and contributing directly to the department‚Äôs day to day operations. They also serve as role models in the community, setting a standard of professionalism and preparedness as they work toward becoming Senior Scam Investigators.
Senior Scam Investigator (SSI)
SSIs hold the second-highest rank in the department and are among the most knowledgeable team members. They are part of the SI Management Team and assist the HSI with high level decisions that affect the entire department. SSIs are responsible for overseeing Scam Investigators, training TSIs, managing disciplinary matters when needed, and handling cases. This role prepares them for potential promotion to Head Scam Investigator.
Head Scam Investigator (HSI)
The HSI is the highest ranking member of the Scam Investigation Department and holds the most trusted leadership position within the team. Selection for this role is highly competitive and based on experience, leadership, and reliability. The HSI is responsible for managing the entire department, overseeing all disciplinary actions, and reporting directly to the Head Admin and Server Manager. They also collaborate with other department heads to ensure smooth server wide operations and to propose improvements for the SI team.

BASIC INVESTIGATION FRAMEWORK

1. Failure to pay after work is completed.

This constitutes a serious breach of the agreement.
Investigate the reasons for non-payment thoroughly. This involves gathering all relevant information from both the developer and the client. Understand the client's perspective and the specific reasons they are withholding payment. Document all communication and any supporting evidence provided by either party.
Assess the work quality meticulously against the originally provided specifications, detailed requirements, or agreed-upon references. Conduct a thorough evaluation to determine if the delivered work demonstrably fails to meet the established standards. If objective evidence indicates a significant deficiency or deviation from the agreed terms, payment may be justifiably withheld, either partially or fully, depending on the extent of the inadequacy. Ensure this assessment is impartial and based on clear, documented criteria.
Evaluate any explanations provided by the client comprehensively, referencing established community guidelines, terms of service, and any prior agreements. Scrutinize the client's justifications for non-payment against the relevant rules and precedents. Determine if the reasons provided are valid and justifiable within the established framework.
Attempt to negotiate a partial payment as a compromise if a full resolution, including full payment, is initially unattainable. Explore the possibility of reaching a mutually acceptable agreement where the developer receives a portion of the agreed-upon payment. This could be considered in situations where the work has some value but may not fully meet all original specifications or where mitigating circumstances exist. Document the negotiation process and any agreed-upon terms. 
Consider offering the developer the opportunity to make revisions if the work is below expectations. However, the client is not obligated to request or accept revisions and may choose not to pay or request a refund.  The client generally doesn‚Äôt have to pay if the work is subpar in quality.
Tip: Try to put yourself into the shoes of the developer and consider the payment given to gauge if the work is sufficient or not. 

If the scammer is deemed guilty by your investigation then punish the scammer. The punishment should be proportionate to the severity and impact of the non-payment. The incident log should include a detailed account of the situation, all communication records, the assessment of work quality, the client's explanations, negotiation attempts, and the final decision to ban. This log serves as a reference for future disputes and helps maintain community standards.
2. Failure to complete work in commission.

This scenario represents a failure to fulfill the agreed-upon obligations within a commissioned project, potentially causing inconvenience and loss for the commissioning party.
Request and thoroughly review the reasoning provided by the developer for the non-completion of the commissioned work. Understand the developer's perspective and the specific factors that prevented them from fulfilling their commitment. Gather all relevant information and supporting documentation.
Exercise careful discretion based on the provided explanations and the established community punishment guide. Consider the validity and legitimacy of the reasons for non-completion in relation to the community's rules and expectations. Factors such as unforeseen personal emergencies, genuine technical difficulties, or a clear lack of effort or communication should be weighed differently.
Prioritize finding a resolution to the commission that is fair to both parties. Explore options such as negotiating an extension with revised timelines, finding a replacement developer (with the original developer potentially obligated to contribute to the cost), or agreeing on a partial refund if some work was completed. Aim for a compromise that addresses the concerns of the commissioning party while acknowledging any extenuating circumstances faced by the developer.
If no resolution is found then punish the scammer. 
3. Changing the original deal of a commission (e.g., terms, payment).

Unilateral changes to the agreed-upon terms of a commission undermine trust and can lead to disputes.
Refer directly to the original agreement established between the involved parties. This agreement, serves as the definitive basis for resolving any discrepancies.
Direct the client who unilaterally changed the terms to provide the agreed upon payment to the affected party to restore the original agreement. 
Punish the scammer if they refuse to resolve the conflict by providing the required agreed upon payment(if it‚Äôs the client) and adhering to the original terms(both). The severity of the punishment should reflect the degree of the violation and the refusal to rectify the situation. Document all actions taken.
4. Blocking or Refusal to respond to a partner in a commission.

Lack of communication and blocking obstruct collaboration and can be indicative of an unwillingness to fulfill obligations.
Inquire with the user who has blocked or refused to respond to their partner to understand the reasons for the lack of communication. Seek their explanation and any relevant context for their actions.
Actively facilitate a resolution to the conflict by mediating between the involved parties. Encourage open communication and attempt to understand the underlying issues. Punish the accused user according to established guidelines if they do not have a legitimate and justifiable reason for their refusal to respond and their lack of cooperation is hindering the project or commission.
5. Delivering subpar work in commission.
The delivery of work that does not meet reasonable standards of quality can lead to dissatisfaction and disputes.
Exercise careful and objective discretion when evaluating the quality of the delivered work, talk with other SIs about their opinions. Avoid subjective biases and focus on demonstrable shortcomings.
Determine if the client provided specific references, detailed instructions, examples, or artistic inspiration prior to the commencement of the work. Clear communication of expectations and provision of reference materials sets a benchmark for quality.
Thoroughly compare the delivered work against any provided references, instructions, or agreed-upon standards. Identify specific areas where the work falls short of the established expectations.
If no specific references or detailed instructions were provided by the client, and the client's initial request was vague or open to interpretation, the client is generally obligated to provide the agreed-upon payment or can get a refund unless the delivered work is demonstrably and significantly below a reasonable standard of quality for the type of commission. In such cases, the burden of clearly communicating expectations largely rests with the client. However, developers are still expected to deliver work that demonstrates a reasonable level of skill and effort. Each situation should be evaluated on its own merits, considering the clarity of the initial agreement.
Consider offering the developer the opportunity to make revisions if the work is below expectations. However, the client is not obligated to request or accept revisions and may choose not to pay or request a refund.  The client generally doesn‚Äôt have to pay if the work is subpar in quality.

Carefully assess the provided reasoning and attempt to reinstate the affected party to the revenue payout if the justification for cancellation is deemed insufficient, unclear, or not strongly supported by evidence and community guidelines. Advocate for the affected user if the removal appears unwarranted or disproportionate to any potential infraction.
Punish the party responsible for the removal if they refuse to reinstate the revenue payout without a valid and clearly substantiated reason. Ensure fairness and adherence to established policies in all revenue payout decisions.
